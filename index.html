The Electrostatic Potential

The Orbitrap is an electrostatic ion trap that confines ions using a quadro-logarithmic electrostatic potential field (often called the Kingdon potential):

U(r, z) = k/2 · (z2 - r2/2) + k/2 · Rm2 · ln(r / Rm) + C

Where r and z are cylindrical coordinates, Rm is the characteristic radius, and k is the axial field curvature, which is directly proportional to the applied trapping voltage.

Decoupled Equations of Motion

Because there are no cross-terms containing both r and z in the potential, the forces in the axial and radial directions are completely decoupled:

  • Axial Restoring Force (Harmonic):
    The axial force is linear with distance from the center: Fz = -q · k · z. The equation of motion is:
    m · d2z/dt2 = -q · k · z
    This results in Simple Harmonic Motion along the z-axis!
  • Axial Oscillation Frequency:
    Solving the harmonic oscillator equation yields the frequency:
    ωz = √(q · k / m)
    Critical Note: The frequency ωz is independent of the ion's initial position, angle, and energy! It depends only on the mass-to-charge ratio (m/q). This is the foundation of high-resolution Orbitrap mass spectrometry.
  • Radial Centrifugal Balance:
    In the radial plane (x-y), the electrostatic attraction towards the spindle balances the centrifugal force, trapping the ions in rosette-like orbits. Stable orbits must exist in the region r < Rm.

Electrodynamic Trapping

If ions were injected into a static full-voltage field, they would return to the injection point and strike the injector. By ramping the voltage (increasing k) immediately after injection, the potential well deepens, causing the orbits to contract and trapping the ions securely.

Differential Detection & FT

The outer electrode is split in half along the z = 0 plane. As positive ions oscillate axially, they induce alternating image currents on the two halves. The differential amplifier measures this current:

I(t) ∝ ∑ qi · vz, i(t)

Taking the Fourier Transform (FT) of this time-domain signal converts it into a frequency spectrum, which is then mapped directly to a mass spectrum since m/z ∝ 1 / f2.
